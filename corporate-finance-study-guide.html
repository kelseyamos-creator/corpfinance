Exam Tips & Common Traps

Price risk: Zero coupon bonds have the highest duration for a given maturity → most sensitive to rate changes. Among the options, always pick the longest-maturity, lowest-coupon bond.
Annuity Due vs Ordinary Annuity: Annuity due pays at the beginning of each period. Both PV and FV of annuity due are higher by exactly (1+k). The answer to "which statement is most correct" is always the one saying both PV and FV are higher.
MIRR vs IRR: When IRR > k, MIRR < IRR (MIRR is pulled toward k because it reinvests at k, not IRR). When IRR = k, MIRR = IRR = k. MIRR is always between k and IRR.
PV of perpetual tax shield: Annual shield = D×r_d×T. PV = (D×r_d×T)/r_d = D×T. The interest rate completely cancels — PV depends only on T and D, not the coupon rate. This always trips people up.
Portfolio SD vs weighted average: Portfolio SD < weighted average SD whenever ρ < 1. For the JnJ/Walgreen problem: weighted avg = 18%, actual portfolio SD = 14.11% (at ρ=0.22). This is diversification in action.
AFN with excess capacity: If fixed assets are not at full capacity, do NOT include them in A*/S₀. Calculate S_capacity = Current fixed assets / capacity%. If S₁ ≤ S_capacity, the fixed asset term = 0.
Corporate valuation model: V_equity = V_operations + Non-operating assets − Total debt. "Total debt" = notes payable + long-term debt ONLY. Accounts payable and accruals are NOT subtracted.
Replacement decisions — loss on old equipment: A loss on sale generates a tax saving (inflow), not an outflow. The larger the loss, the larger the tax saving, and the better the project NPV looks. The statement "loss is an outflow and larger loss reduces NPV" is FALSE.
Multi-stage DDM — where to anchor P: The Gordon-model terminal value Pₙ = D_{n+1}/(r–g) must be calculated at the period immediately before the first dividend that grows at constant rate g. Discount Pₙ back n periods to t=0.
NPV vs IRR for mutually exclusive projects: Always choose by NPV for mutually exclusive projects. IRR can mislead when projects differ in size, timing, or when multiple sign changes exist. The A vs B problem: NPV says A wins (€9.02M > €7.04M); IRR also says A (≈17.7% > 17.0%) — they agree here, but that's not always the case.

Agency relationship: one or more persons hire another but withhold decision-making authority
Agency A: True ▼

An agency relationship exists when one or more persons hire another person to perform some service but withhold decision-making authority from that person.

  • a True
  • b False
✓ Answer: A — True

An agency relationship exists whenever one or more persons (the principal) hires another (the agent) to perform a service. The relationship exists regardless of how much decision-making authority is delegated. The question is TRUE because even when authority is withheld — i.e., the agent acts purely as a service provider — the agency relationship still exists.

In finance, the agency problem typically arises when authority is delegated (e.g., shareholders hire managers and give them discretion over investment decisions), because the agent may then act in their own interest rather than the principal's. Agency costs are the expenses incurred to monitor agents or align their incentives — through compensation contracts, audits, or the threat of takeover.

Portfolio risk is NOT necessarily a weighted average of security standard deviations
Portfolio A: True ▼

While the portfolio return is a weighted average of realized security returns, portfolio risk is not necessarily a weighted average of the standard deviations of the securities in the portfolio. It is this aspect of portfolios that allows investors to combine stocks and actually reduce the riskiness of a portfolio.

  • a True
  • b False
✓ Answer: A — True

Portfolio return = weighted average of individual returns. However, portfolio risk (σ) depends on the correlations between securities. When correlation < 1, diversification reduces risk below the weighted average of individual σs. This is the mathematical basis of diversification benefits.

σ_portfolio < Σ(w_i × σ_i) when correlations < 1

Ordinary annuity vs annuity due — $10,000/yr for 5 years, which statement is correct?
TVM Ans: C ▼

Suppose someone offered you the choice of two equally risky annuities, each paying $10,000 per year for five years. One is an ordinary (or deferred) annuity, the other is an annuity due. Which of the following statements is most correct?

  • a The present value of the ordinary annuity must exceed the present value of the annuity due, but the future value of an ordinary annuity may be less than the future value of the annuity due.
  • b The present value of the annuity due exceeds the present value of the ordinary annuity, while the future value of the annuity due is less than the future value of the ordinary annuity.
  • c The present value of the annuity due exceeds the present value of the ordinary annuity, and the future value of the annuity due also exceeds the future value of the ordinary annuity.
  • d If interest rates increase, the difference between the present value of the ordinary annuity and the present value of the annuity due remains the same.
  • e Both answers a and d are correct.
✓ Answer: C

Annuity due pays at the beginning of each period; ordinary annuity pays at the end. Since all payments are one period earlier for annuity due, both PV and FV are higher by a factor of (1+k):

PV_due = PV_ordinary × (1 + k)
FV_due = FV_ordinary × (1 + k)

So both exceed the ordinary annuity values — answer C is correct. Statement b is wrong because FV of annuity due is also higher, not lower.

Which of the following has the greatest price risk?
Bonds Ans: C ▼

Which of the following has the greatest price risk?

  • a A 10-year, $1,000 face value, 10% coupon bond with semi-annual interest payments.
  • b A 10-year, $1,000 face value, 10% coupon bond with annual interest payments.
  • c A 10-year, $1,000 face value, zero coupon bond.
  • d A 10-year $100 annuity.
  • e All of the above have the same price risk since they all mature in 10 years.
✓ Answer: C — Zero coupon bond has highest price risk

Price risk (interest rate sensitivity) is driven by duration. A zero coupon bond has the maximum possible duration for a given maturity because all cash flow comes at maturity. Coupon bonds return cash earlier, reducing duration below maturity. The semi-annual coupon bond (a) has slightly lower duration than the annual coupon (b), since it returns cash even more frequently. The zero coupon bond (c) has duration = maturity = 10 years, the highest of the group.

Given the following data:

k* = real risk-free rate = 4%

Maturity risk premium = 1%

Euler Ltd bonds — 8 years remaining, bankruptcy postponement, PV at 28% required return
Bonds Ans: D ▼

You are the owner of 100 bonds issued by Euler, Ltd. These bonds have 8 years remaining to maturity, an annual coupon payment of $80, and a par value of $1,000. Unfortunately, Euler is on the brink of bankruptcy. The creditors, including yourself, have agreed to a postponement of the next 4 interest payments (otherwise, the next interest payment would have been due in 1 year). The remaining interest payments, for Years 5 through 8, will be made as scheduled. The postponed payments will accrue interest at an annual rate of 6 percent, and they will then be paid as a lump sum at maturity 8 years hence. The required rate of return on these bonds, considering their substantial risk, is now 28 percent. What is the present value of each bond?

  • a $538.21
  • b $426.73
  • c $384.84
  • d $266.88
  • e $249.98
✓ Answer: D — $266.88

Step 1: Find the lump sum of deferred payments at Year 8. Payments of $80 deferred for 4 years accrue at 6% per year:

Deferred lump sum at Year 8 = $80 × [(1.06)⁴ + (1.06)³ + (1.06)² + (1.06)¹]
= $80 × [1.2625 + 1.1910 + 1.1236 + 1.06]
= $80 × 4.6371 = $370.97

Step 2: Cash flows at k=28%: Years 5–8 get $80 coupon each. Year 8 also gets $1,000 par + $370.97 deferred. Years 1–4: $0.

PV = 80/(1.28)⁵ + 80/(1.28)⁶ + 80/(1.28)⁷ + (80+1000+370.97)/(1.28)⁸
≈ $266.88

Stock A (β=0.8) vs Stock B (β=1.6) — which statements are most correct?
CAPM Ans: A ▼

Stock A has a beta equal to 0.8, while stock B has a beta equal to 1.6. Which of the following statements is most correct?

  • a Stock B's required rate of return is double that of stock A's.
  • b An equally weighted portfolio of stock A and stock B will have a beta less than 1.2.
  • c If market participants become more risk averse, the required rate of return on stock B will increase more than the required rate of return for stock A.
  • d All the statements above are correct.
  • e Statements a and c are correct.
✓ Answer: A

By CAPM: r_s = r_RF + β(r_M − r_RF). If the risk premium (r_M − r_RF) is the same for both stocks, then r_B / r_A = β_B / β_A = 1.6 / 0.8 = 2 only if r_RF = 0. In the general case, statement a is technically only true when r_RF = 0 — the official key marks A, indicating the exam accepts this under the simplifying assumption. An equally-weighted portfolio beta = (0.8 + 1.6)/2 = 1.2, exactly (not less than 1.2), so b is false. C is true as a standalone. The official key is A.

Multi-stage growth stock: D₀=$5, 20% for 10yr, then 15% for 10yr, then 10% forever — price at 10% required return?
Stocks Ans: E ▼

A share of stock has a dividend of D₀ = $5. The dividend is expected to grow at a 20 percent annual rate for the next 10 years, then at a 15 percent rate for 10 more years, and then at a long-run normal growth rate of 10 percent forever. If investors require a 10 percent return on this stock, what is its current price?

  • a $100.00
  • b $ 82.35
  • c $195.50
  • d $212.62
  • e The data given in the problem are internally inconsistent — the situation described is impossible in that no equilibrium price can be produced.
✓ Answer: E — Data inconsistent

The Gordon Growth Model requires g < k (required return) for a finite price to exist. For the first 10 years, g = 20% > k = 10%. For years 11–20, g = 15% > k = 10%. The stable growth rate g = 10% = k, which also leads to an undefined (infinite) price in the Gordon model (denominator = 0). Therefore, no finite equilibrium price can exist — the data are internally inconsistent.

NPV profiles crossing in upper-left quadrant at −10% — is there an NPV vs IRR conflict?
NPV/IRR Ans: B ▼

Your assistant has just completed an analysis of two mutually exclusive projects. You must now take her report to a board of directors meeting and present the alternatives for the board's consideration. To help you with your presentation, your assistant also constructed a graph with NPV profiles for the two projects. However, she forgot to label the profiles, so you do not know which line applies to which project. Of the following statements regarding the profiles, which one is most reasonable?

  • a If the two projects have the same investment cost, and if their NPV profiles cross once in the upper right quadrant, at a discount rate of 40 percent, this suggests that a NPV versus IRR conflict is not likely to exist.
  • b If the two projects' NPV profiles cross once, in the upper left quadrant, at a discount rate of minus 10 percent, then there will probably not be a NPV versus IRR conflict irrespective of the relative sizes of the two projects, in any meaningful, practical sense (that is, a conflict which will affect the actual investment decision).
  • c If one of the projects has a NPV profile which crosses the X-axis twice, hence the project appears to have two IRRs, your assistant must have made a mistake.
  • d Whenever a conflict between NPV and IRR exist, then, if the two projects have the same initial cost, the one with the steeper NPV profile probably has less rapid cash flows.
  • e If the two projects both have a single outlay at t = 0, followed by a series of positive cash inflows, and if their NPV profiles cross in the lower left quadrant, then one of the projects should be accepted, and both would be accepted if they were not mutually exclusive.
✓ Answer: B

If the crossover rate is negative (e.g., −10%), it lies outside the relevant region of discount rates (which must be ≥ 0 in practice). Therefore, over the entire feasible range of discount rates, the NPV ranking of the projects stays the same — whichever has higher NPV at 0% maintains that ranking for all positive rates. This means an NPV vs IRR conflict is practically impossible.

Risk, Return & Portfolio Theory

Standard deviation, coefficient of variation, portfolio variance, diversification, correlation

Two key separations: (1) Standalone vs portfolio context. For a standalone asset, use CV (= σ/μ) to compare risk per unit of return. For assets in a portfolio, use beta — only systematic risk is rewarded. (2) Return vs risk. Portfolio return = weighted average of individual returns. Portfolio risk (σ) is NOT a weighted average — it depends on correlations, and is always ≤ weighted average when ρ < 1.

Key formula — 2-asset portfolio variance:
σ²_p = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂

When IRR = k: MIRR = IRR = k
When IRR < k: MIRR > IRR (both below k, MIRR closer to k)

T1

T3
Why is the PV of an annuity due always higher than the PV of an ordinary annuity — even when the total payments are identical?

The payments are identical in size and number — the only difference is timing. In an annuity due, every payment arrives at the beginning of its period rather than the end. T8
A stock has very high total volatility but a beta close to zero. Should it earn a high expected return?

This is the central message of CAPM: only systematic risk is priced. T9

  • a There is no flaw — higher IRR always means better project.
  • b IRR is a percentage return and ignores scale. Project B generates far more absolute value (NPV) despite a lower percentage return. A 20% return on €100M creates more wealth than a 25% return on €10M.
  • c The flaw is that MIRR should be used instead of IRR in all cases.

Why does MIRR solve one of IRR's main problems — and what assumption does MIRR make instead?

T14
If debt is so valuable because of tax shields, why don't firms borrow as much as possible?

Under Modigliani-Miller with taxes, every euro of debt creates value through interest tax shields. A student concludes firms should therefore maximise debt. What is missing from this reasoning?

  • b MM with taxes is incomplete.   • c The tax shield is fixed regardless of debt level, so additional debt creates no additional benefit.

This is why the exam includes both the 35% and 21% versions of the Arnell problem — it is testing whether you understand that the tax rate, not the interest rate, is the key variable.

T17
A firm uses its overall WACC to evaluate a new division that is far riskier than its existing business. What error does this introduce?
WACC / Risk-adjustment ▼

A conservative utility company (WACC = 8%) acquires a technology startup division. It evaluates the startup's projects at 8%. The startup's standalone WACC would be approximately 20%. What systematic error does the firm make, and what is the long-run consequence?

  • a No error — WACC should be the same across all divisions for consistency.
  • b The firm will accept too many risky projects (discounting at 8% makes risky projects look better than they are) and reject too many safe ones. Over time, the firm's overall risk profile increases and actual WACC rises.
  • c The firm will reject too many risky projects because 8% is too conservative for a startup.
  • d There is no consequence — WACC differences between divisions are a theoretical concern only.
Answer: B

This is exactly the McCue Company problem from the Example Exam (Q x). A single WACC applied to projects of different risk creates systematic mispricing. Low-risk projects (true required return perhaps 5%) look worse when discounted at 8%. The firm therefore systematically accepts risky projects and rejects safe ones. Over time, the portfolio of projects becomes riskier, investors demand a higher return, the actual WACC rises, and the firm finds itself with assets that are less valuable than expected. The solution is to use risk-adjusted hurdle rates: estimate a beta for each division and apply CAPM to find the appropriate discount rate.

Under MM dividend irrelevance, a firm paying no dividends should be worth the same as an identical firm paying large dividends. Why?

  • b Investors can create their own dividend policy. If the firm doesn't pay dividends, an investor who wants cash can sell shares.   • b Not necessarily. By not paying on day 10, the firm pays 2% extra to keep the cash for 20 more days — an annualised cost of roughly 37%. This is an extremely expensive form of financing. If the firm can borrow at less than 37%, it should borrow and take the discount.

Bondi Corporation has $20 million in permanent outstanding debt with an interest rate of 5%. The corporate tax rate is 28%.

  • What is the annual interest tax shield?
  • What is the present value of the perpetual tax shield?
  • Suppose interest rates rise and Bondi refinances at 7%. How does the PV of the tax shield change?
✓ Solutions
a) Annual shield = D × r_d × T = 20M × 0.05 × 0.28 = $280,000

b) PV = Annual shield / r_d = 280,000 / 0.05 = $5,600,000
OR directly: PV = T × D = 0.28 × 20M = $5,600,000 ← much faster

c) At 7%: Annual shield = 20M × 0.07 × 0.28 = $392,000
PV = 392,000 / 0.07 = $5,600,000
→ Unchanged. PV = T × D regardless of the interest rate.

This is one of the most important results in corporate finance — and it always surprises people. The interest rate completely cancels out of the PV formula. Only the tax rate and the debt amount matter. A firm with $20M of debt at 5% has an identical tax shield to a firm with $20M at 7%. This is why the 2017 US tax reform (35% → 21%) reduced tax shield values by 40% — the rate dropped. The interest rate didn't change the shield; the tax rate did.

According to pecking order theory, in what order should firms raise capital — and why is equity the last resort?
Theory — Pecking Order Internal → Debt → Equity ▼

Which of the following best describes the pecking order theory of capital structure financing, and the correct rationale for each preference?

  • a Firms prefer equity first because it involves no fixed obligations, then debt, then retained earnings.
  • b Firms prefer debt first because the tax shield makes it cheapest, then retained earnings, then equity.
  • c Firms prefer internal funds first (no information asymmetry cost), then debt (signals confidence, limited adverse selection), then equity last (signals overvaluation, triggers price drop).
  • d Firms are indifferent between sources of capital — the pecking order only applies to financially distressed firms.
✓ Answer: C

The pecking order arises from information asymmetry — managers know more about firm value than outside investors. Internal funds (retained earnings) have no adverse selection problem — no new securities are issued, so no signal is sent. Debt is next: issuing debt signals that management is confident enough in future cash flows to service fixed obligations, and investors have a contractual claim that partially protects them from overvaluation. Equity is the worst signal: if managers are willing to issue equity, rational investors infer the stock must be overvalued (otherwise managers would use cheaper debt). The result: equity issuance depresses the stock price, making it the most expensive source of capital in practice — which is why firms avoid it.

A stock consistently earns above the SML prediction for 3 years. Which explanation is most consistent with semistrong market efficiency?
Theory — Efficiency & CAPM Beta is mis-estimated ▼

A fund's quantitative model shows that a particular stock has consistently earned returns above what CAPM predicts (above the SML) for three consecutive years. The fund manager concludes the market is inefficient and the stock is systematically mispriced. Which explanation is most consistent with semistrong-form market efficiency?

  • a The market is definitely inefficient — three years of outperformance proves it.
  • b The manager got lucky — random variation can produce three years of outperformance.
  • c The stock's beta is likely mis-estimated. If the true systematic risk is higher than measured, the required return should be higher — and the apparent excess return disappears once beta is corrected.
  • d The stock has a unique product that makes it immune to market forces.
✓ Answer: C

Apparent violations of market efficiency are often the result of measurement error in beta, not true mispricing. CAPM uses a single market index as a proxy for the true "market portfolio" — but the true market portfolio includes all risky assets worldwide (human capital, real estate, private equity, etc.), and the measured beta is an approximation. If the stock has exposure to risk factors not captured by the market index (e.g., liquidity risk, size premium, value premium), the measured beta understates true systematic risk, and the apparent outperformance is actually appropriate compensation for omitted risk. The joint hypothesis problem: you can never test market efficiency without simultaneously testing the asset pricing model used to define "fair" return.

= 180 + 70 – 140 – 15
= €95 million

FCF (€95M) is lower than net income (€180M) because CapEx and working capital investment together absorbed €85M more than depreciation returned.

Which of the following statements about MIRR is FALSE?
Theory — MIRR Statement C is FALSE ▼

Which of the following statements about the Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR) is FALSE?

  • a MIRR assumes that all positive cash flows are reinvested at the cost of capital (WACC), which is more realistic than IRR's assumption.
  • b When a project's IRR exceeds the WACC, the MIRR will be lower than the IRR.
  • c MIRR and IRR will always give the same accept/reject decision for a project with normal cash flows, but MIRR always gives a higher numerical value than IRR.
  • d MIRR resolves the problem of multiple IRRs that arises when a project's cash flows change sign more than once.
✓ Answer: C is FALSE

Statement C contains two parts — the first is correct (MIRR and IRR give the same accept/reject decision for normal cash flows), but the second is wrong. MIRR does NOT always give a higher numerical value than IRR. Specifically: when IRR > WACC (a good project), reinvesting at the lower WACC produces less terminal value, so MIRR < IRR. When IRR < WACC (a bad project), MIRR > IRR. When IRR = WACC, MIRR = IRR. So the relationship between MIRR and IRR depends entirely on how IRR compares to WACC — it is not a fixed direction. Statements a, b, and d are all correct descriptions of MIRR.
